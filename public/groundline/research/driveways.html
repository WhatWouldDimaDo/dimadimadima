3 · Demand & urgency signals

Replacement triggers [sourced]: alligator/spiderweb cracking, cracks >1/4", sunken or heaved sections, drainage/standing water, driveway pulling away from garage slab, age past the 25–30 year concrete lifespan. (Concrete Network, The Driveway Company, Sam The Concrete Man blog)

Sale-prep angle [sourced]: driveway replacement returns 50–80% ROI at resale; paver driveways the top of that range (70–80%); 72% of buyers say curb appeal shapes first impression; agents rank curb appeal a primary price factor. (Angi, We Love Paving, Clever) → pre-listing is a real secondary trigger, and the GSCCCA just-sold deed index [prior work] works here the same way it does for paint.

Seasonality [sourced]: ideal pour windows are spring and fall — 50–90°F sustained; Sept–early Oct is the Georgia sweet spot; deep summer (rapid cure, surface cracking) and winter (cure halt) are avoided. (Angi, Pete's Crete) → a late-August campaign drop targets the fall pour window almost perfectly. [estimated timing implication]

Considered vs. distress [estimated]: this is a slow-decay considered purchase, not distress-driven. A cracked driveway is embarrassing but never an emergency (contrast: roof leak). That's actually favorable for the playbook — the homeowner has been passively annoyed for years, nobody has ever knocked with a photo of their driveway fixed, and there's no insurance-claim chaos or storm-chaser noise to compete with. The downside of no-urgency is longer decision cycles; the printed piece + render has to manufacture the trigger.

4 · Targeting feasibility — the core question

Short version: this is the most machine-targetable vertical in the portfolio. Driveway condition is objective, street-facing, and high-contrast — unlike landscaping quality (taste) or roof condition (needs aerial).

What's proven in the literature [sourced]

  • Road-surface CV is a mature field. Deep-learning pavement distress detection has been standard since ~2015; YOLO-family models trained on street-view imagery detect and classify cracking types directly from Google Street View frames (YOLOv5s-M, Int. J. Applied Earth Obs. 2023; end-to-end distress detection + quantification, Constr. Bldg. Mat. 2024). One published dataset was built from 7,237 GSV-extracted images.
  • The exact pipeline needed was published as a proof-of-concept in 2026: fully automated GSV image acquisition over a user-defined polygon + zero-shot vision-language-model distress mapping — no custom model training at all (Transportation Research Interdisciplinary Perspectives, 2026). That is: draw Buckhead polygon → pull frames → ask a VLM "rate the pavement" → map. On roads, not driveways — but the visual task is the same material and same distress types.
  • VLM benchmarking exists and is encouraging: a 2026 PLOS One study benchmarked 7 multimodal models (Gemini 2.5 Pro, GPT-4o, o1, + 4 open-source) on distress identification, severity evaluation, and maintenance estimation — conclusion: MLLMs can interpret street-level imagery for pavement condition cost-effectively, GPT-4o recommended for balance (PLOS One). A PCI-outputting VLM ("PaveGPT") produces ASTM D6433-consistent severity reasoning (arXiv).
  • Satellite/oblique is the weaker channel for cracks: best commercial aerial (Nearmap HyperCamera3) is 4.4–7.5 cm/pixel, refreshed up to 3×/yr (Nearmap) — enough for roof-level condition scoring (their AI does 40+ roof attributes) and enough to see driveway material, footprint, staining, and major settlement, but hairline-to-1/4" cracks are at or below pixel scale. [sourced resolution, estimated implication] Nearmap sells no driveway-condition layer today (roof-focused). Use aerial for driveway size/shape measurement (quote pre-fill), not distress detection.

What transfers vs. what doesn't [estimated]

Transfers cleanly: distress taxonomy (alligator cracking, longitudinal/transverse cracks, patches, spalling) is identical for residential concrete/asphalt; GSV camera angle actually favors driveways (perpendicular view from the street, vs. oblique down-road view for pavement).
Doesn't transfer: published models are trained on asphalt roadway; residential is mostly broom-finish concrete with expansion joints that a naive model could read as cracks. This is why zero-shot VLM ("rate this driveway 1–5, ignore expansion joints") is the right first tool, not a fine-tuned YOLO.

Prior reusable findings [prior work]

  • Street View Static API ≈ $7/1,000 images — a whole 500-home sweep costs <$5.
  • Real gate is image freshness: 1–5+ yr GSV re-shoot cycles. Freshness risk is worse for driveways than landscaping: a driveway replaced since the last GSV pass makes the mailer actively embarrassing ("your damaged driveway" → they fixed it last year). Mitigation: GSV metadata returns capture date free of charge — filter to ≤24-month imagery, and pair with Nearmap/Google aerial (fresher) as a cross-check that the surface hasn't visibly changed. [estimated mitigation]
  • ToS ambiguity on AI-transformed marketing use of GSV imagery unresolved — but Scaped.ai runs exactly this (GSV → AI filter → render → postcard) commercially at stated 4.2% response rates (scaped.ai). Same risk posture as the landscaping work; no new exposure.

Recommended validation before any pilot [estimated]

A half-day, ~$1 benchmark: pull 30 GSV frames of known driveways (10 visibly bad, 10 fine, 10 borderline — hand-picked by driving Morningside/Buckhead or from the contractor's own quote log), have Claude/Gemini score condition 1–5 with a rubric (ignore expansion joints, flag material type, flag visibility obstructions), compare to human labels. Gate: ≥80% agreement on bad-vs-fine, zero "fine" homes scored 4–5. This mirrors the hand-built golden-set-first rule from the parks project.

Read: the general national pattern — in-kind replacement exempt, curb cut always permitted (PermitMint guide) — holds here. Most driveway replacements will never appear in a permit portal, so the permit scraper is NOT the signal engine for this vertical. Two residual permit uses: (a) ROW/apron permits in City of Atlanta as a sparse competitor-activity feed, (b) other-trade permits (pool, addition, new build) as adjacent signals — heavy equipment destroys driveways, and a just-finished pool build is a classic driveway-replacement moment [estimated, consistent with the Jul 20 Shovels adjacent-signal finding].

This is exactly the vertical where the CV/imagery signal replaces the permit signal — the damage itself is the public record, sitting in Street View.

6 · Competitive check

  • CurbLead — confirmed from the Jul 20 deep-dive: hardscape/pavers vertical explicitly includes driveways (patios, driveways, walkways, retaining walls, stamped concrete). But it's render-forward ("dream upgrade"), pay-per-piece postcards, pre-launch/no verifiable customers, and there's no evidence it does damage detection or condition-based targeting — targeting modes are address-list paste, pin+radius, and neighbor-saturation.
  • Scaped.ai — commercially live, GSV-based, "identifies homes that need landscaping or hardscaping work using AI filtering" then mails render postcards; claims 4.2% response (scaped.ai). Closest to condition-aware targeting, but landscaping/hardscape-branded, and postcard-only — no address-specific proposal site, no door delivery.
  • Roomagen — free consumer AI tool that renders paver/concrete/cobblestone driveways onto property photos (roomagen.com) — evidence render quality for driveways is a solved problem, not a competitor.
  • Nobody found doing "damage-ranked" driveway lead-gen. Every player renders the upgrade; none scores the existing damage and leads with it. The "50 most damaged driveways in Buckhead" framing — an objective, ranked, evidence-backed list — appears unoccupied. That framing is also exactly what made the contractor light up: it's a sales weapon ("I know your driveway is the 4th worst on this street"), not just a mailer. [sourced absence — can't prove a negative, but three competitor deep-dives + fresh searches surfaced no one]

The call

Conditional go. Driveways don't beat roofing on ticket or trigger, and the permit-trail criterion outright fails — but the vertical has the single strongest targeting story in the entire portfolio, an unoccupied "damage-ranked list" positioning no competitor touches, a warm anchor contractor already sold on the hook, and near-zero validation cost. It's also the cleanest technical demo of the whole thesis: "we found the 50 worst driveways in Buckhead from public imagery" is a one-sentence proof that timing/targeting beats spray-and-pray.

Conditions, in order:

  1. Run the 30-frame VLM benchmark (half day, ~$1) before promising the contractor anything. Gate: ≥80% bad-vs-fine agreement, no false "damaged" calls on fine driveways, expansion joints handled.
  2. Confirm the anchor's paver capability and appetite — if he's concrete-replacement-only, fee economics are marginal ($600–800/job vs. real delivery effort); the vertical's upside is the $15–45K paver upsell.
  3. Sequencing per the standing rule: stays behind the Creekside pilot T0 read-out. The one exception worth considering: the VLM benchmark (condition 1) is pure desk research with no client-facing commitment — it can run any time without violating the gate.
  4. Freshness discipline: ship nothing against GSV imagery older than 24 months without an aerial or drive-by cross-check — a "fix your damaged driveway" piece to a house that already fixed it burns the trusted-referral positioning the whole thesis rests on.

What would upgrade this to a full go: benchmark passes + anchor confirms paver mix + one campaign produces a paver-tier close. What would kill it: VLM can't beat ~80% agreement even with rubric iteration (unlikely per the published benchmarks, but that's what the test is for), or the anchor turns out concrete-only with no interest in upsell.
