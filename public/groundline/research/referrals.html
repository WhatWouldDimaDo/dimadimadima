Referral program design — Creekside

← back to research

For: Creekside Premier Landscaping (Dunwoody) · Status: working design for owner review — 3 open decisions at bottom.
Trigger: referrals are the quickest path — and use AI itself to design the structure.
Evidence base: 292-review Google Maps corpus, 91-thread Reddit deep dive, 7-thread practitioner insights, plus external benchmarks cited inline.

Design principles (from the corpus — every mechanic below traces to one of these)

  1. Named-person accountability is the #1 five-star driver. 140+ of 292 reviews name a specific person ("Nathan brought our vision to life," "Ignacio and his crew"). Referrals must credit and route to a named person, never "the company."
  2. Ghosting is the #1 trust-breaker — the worst review in the corpus is a $25K quote ghosted after a promised rendering. Customers extrapolate: "if it's like that now, it's not going to get better." Every referral lead gets a same-day response from a named person, enforced as an SLA.
  3. Reputation beats price-shopping at this tier. Clients explicitly skip competitive bids on referral ("expertise was more important than price... didn't secure other quotes"). Referral leads close at premium margins — worth real program spend.
  4. Tangible branded gifts beat cash discounts at $25–75K tickets ("a Christmas wreath, not $50 off"). Discounts also contaminate a premium price position.
  5. Proximity + social proof converts 3–5× cold outreach (door hangers on just-finished neighbors, per multiple independent Reddit threads).
  6. Understatement wins with affluent buyers — over-advertising reads as incompetence ("I'm not paying for your truck payment"). Small tasteful signs, quality gifts, word-of-mouth posture.
  7. Speed-to-quote beats price on close rate (~30% self-reported conversion lift from 24-hr quote + 48-hr follow-up; "first to respond, not cheapest, wins").

Component 1 — Past-customer referral loop

Reward structure

Referrer side — tangible, branded, seasonal. Never a discount.

Sizing rationale: external benchmarks put high-ticket home-services referral rewards at $100–200 typical, up to $1,000 for completed remodel projects (ReferralRock, US Tech Automations, Referral Factory). Against a $25–75K average ticket and shared-lead costs of $80–100+ per unclosed, 4-way-shared lead (Reddit corpus), a ~$300 gift for a closed exclusive job is cheap. Service credits reportedly outperform cash by ~18% because they drive repeat business — the "seasonal refresh visit" option captures that without ever discounting.

Referred-friend side — the gift is the AI render, not a discount. The referred neighbor gets: same-day call from the named person + a complimentary AI concept render of their own yard within 48 hours of the walkthrough. This sits below the entire $300–$5,000 human design-fee ladder in cost but carries visible design value, and since it's absorbed as marketing (never billed), the entire design-fee/IP-anxiety complaint category from the corpus disappears.

Ask timing — keyed to the review/completion moment

  • Ask #1 — final walkthrough day. Same moment as the review ask (text-with-link review requests see ~60% uptake per practitioner reports). The named crew lead makes the ask in person, then the follow-up text carries both links. Script for the lead: "If a neighbor asks who did the yard — and they will — here's my card, or just text them my number."
  • Ask #2 — the compliment moment. Reviews literally say "we regularly receive compliments on our yard." 30–60 days post-install, when the landscape has matured, the named person texts: a photo of the finished work + "hope you're getting compliments — if anyone asks, I'd love to take care of them personally."
  • Ask #3 — seasonal. The gift deliveries themselves (wreath, refresh visit) are the recurring ask surface. No standalone "please refer us" blasts.

Named-person mechanic

  • Every referral card / text / co-branded page carries a person's name and direct line (see Open Decision 3 for whose).
  • Inbound referral leads route to that named person; that person makes the same-day contact — not office admin. The corpus's richest 5-star reviews ("Victoria in the office is an absolute gem," "Chris Noonan made me feel heard") all start at first contact.
  • Internal: the named person gets visible credit per closed referral (recognition + spiff, structure TBD by the owners). This makes crew leads want to make Ask #1.

Component 2 — Dormant-base reactivation (the acquired book)

The dormant base is the single cheapest pipeline Creekside owns: reactivation costs 5–7× less than new acquisition, and existing customers convert at 60–70% vs 5–20% for cold (Nutshell, PGM Solutions). Physical mail — especially something dimensional — outperforms digital for win-back at this tier.

The framing decision: "new owners, same crews" — handled honestly

The ownership change is not something to hide; it's the news hook that justifies the outreach. A generic marketing blast from a company these people thought they knew reads as spam. "We bought the company Scott built, and we're reinvesting in it" reads as news.

The honesty gate: only claim "same crews" if it's true. If the crews largely carried over, lead with that — crew continuity is the strongest trust anchor available ("treated our space like it was their own" is about crews, not owners). If crews turned over, do not manufacture continuity; lead instead with "same standards, new investment" and let the named-person mechanic rebuild trust person by person. This is Open Decision 1 — the drafts below assume crew continuity is at least partially true and flag the line to cut if not.

Message drafts (voice built from the 20-phrase language bank — clients' own words, not marketing-speak)

Draft 1 — Segment A letter (physical, owner-signed, includes a printed AI render of their property):

Dear [First name],

A few years ago, Creekside [built the stone patio / redid the front beds — pull the actual job from the file] at [street name]. We're the new owners — Scott built something worth continuing, and earlier this year we bought the company from him to do exactly that. Same crews you'd recognize (cut this clause if Decision 1 says no), same standards, and some new tools we're genuinely excited about.

One of those tools made the enclosed image: it's your property, as it looks today, with [one specific idea — e.g., the side yard converted to a lit stone path and native beds]. Not a commitment, not a bill — just what's possible. Clients tell us the best part of a finished project is that they "regularly receive compliments on the yard," and we'd like you to have that again.

If you'd like to walk the property, I'll come personally. My cell is below — you'll get me, not a call center.

[Owner name], Owner, Creekside Premier Landscaping

Draft 2 — Segment B/C email or text (shorter, named person, anti-ghosting posture):

Hi [First name] — [Name] here from Creekside Premier Landscaping. You worked with us back when Scott owned the company; my partner and I took it over this year and I'm personally reaching back out to everyone in the book.

No pitch — just this: if there's anything on your property you've been meaning to deal with, I'll come take a look, and you'll have a real answer (with a picture of what it could look like) within two days of the walkthrough. No chasing us for a callback — that's the whole point of how we're running this company.

Either way, good to be back in touch. — [Name], [cell]

Draft 3 — Segment C follow-up (the never-closed quotes, 10–14 days after Draft 2 if no response):

[First name] — one more note and then I'll leave it be. I found the estimate we prepared for your [project] in [year] that never went anywhere. If the project's still in your head, I'd like a second shot at it: fresh walkthrough, honest range (not a teaser number), and a rendering of the finished result before you decide anything. If the moment's passed, no harm — but if a neighbor ever asks who to call, I'd be grateful for the mention. — [Name]

Sequencing: A first (highest value, physical mail, ~2 weeks before the others so responses don't collide), then B/C. Every response gets the same-day named-person SLA. Non-responders get exactly one follow-up — this tier punishes pestering.

Component 3 — Trade-partner web

The Reddit corpus is unambiguous: pool companies, painters, fence installers, mosquito control, realtors, property managers, and stone/mulch suppliers "are already hired by home owners and those owners trust them." The etiquette standard from a 20-year home-services veteran: "Don't just give out a company's name, tell the customer you will have the company call them" — warm handoffs both directions, always.

Tiered partner structure

Tier 1 — Anchor partners (pool builders; formal, compensated).
First candidate: North Atlanta Pool Services. Pool projects and $25–75K landscaping projects are natural twins (the pool surround is a landscaping job), and ticket sizes justify formal economics.

  • Compensation: trade referral fees conventionally run 5–10% of project value, with remodeling-type work at the lower 3–5% end (Swivl cost guide, Markup and Profit). Recommend opening at 5% of closed contract value, capped, paid at project completion — or an equivalent flat per-closed-job amount if NAPS prefers simplicity (Open Decision 2). On a $40K job the 5–10% norm implies a $2–4K expectation; the cap conversation happens up front, never after (undisclosed pricing, not pricing itself, is what breaks trust — same rule as design fees).
  • Reciprocity: Creekside sends pool-intent leads back with the warm-handoff standard ("NAPS will call you today"), and tracks the two-way ledger transparently in a shared monthly note.
  • What NAPS gets beyond fees (the AI edge): when NAPS quotes a pool, Creekside supplies within 48 hrs a co-branded render of the pool with the finished surround — hardscape, planting, lighting. NAPS closes more pools because the client sees the whole backyard; Creekside is embedded in the proposal before the client ever shops for a landscaper. No other landscaper can offer a pool builder this.

Tier 2 — Adjacent trades (painters, fence, mosquito control, outdoor lighting, irrigation, tree services; informal, gift-based).

  • Per closed referral: a quality tangible thank-you to the individual who referred (~$150–250 range; est.) plus reciprocal referrals. Many small operators prefer reciprocity + recognition over fee paperwork — offer both, let them pick.
  • Crew-level relationships matter as much as owner-level ("if you have a relationship with the company and the technicians, it's your name on the tip of their tongue") — occasional crew lunch drops at active partner job sites are part of the program, not fluff.

Tier 3 — Gatekeepers (realtors, property managers, stone/mulch suppliers; volume, no per-job fees).

  • Realtors: many brokerages restrict cash referral fees, so route value through the client instead — a Creekside "new-home yard package": free walkthrough + AI render for their buyer, presented as the realtor's closing gift, co-branded to the agent. The agent gets a memorable gift at zero cost; Creekside gets warm entry at the exact "new buyers need work done ASAP" moment the Reddit corpus flags.
  • Property managers: single named contact + priority response window (they "hate dealing with multiple contractors" — being the one who always answers is the entire pitch).
  • Stone/mulch suppliers: counter cards, and the same per-referral tangible thank-you as Tier 2 when a counter referral closes.

How AI makes Creekside the easiest partner to refer

The core mechanic: a partner should be able to refer Creekside with one text message. Partner texts an address + one line of context to a dedicated number → within 24 hrs Creekside returns a link to an instant co-branded proposal page: the prospect's own property, an AI concept render, a scope range (a range, never a fake-precise number — practitioners pad estimates by thousands for found conditions, and precision reads as naive), the named person who will call them, and both logos. The partner forwards the link to their client and looks brilliant. Every page is tagged to the partner for attribution and for the monthly two-way ledger — partners resent black-hole referrals as much as customers resent ghosting.

Component 4 — Neighborhood surround (just-finished-job plays)

The evidence: door hangers on neighbors of a just-finished job convert 3–5× cold flyers; the closest AI-render analogue in the field (Scaped.ai postcards) ran 200 pieces → 9 calls → 2 closed jobs including a patio that paid for the campaign. Personalization of the prospect's own house is what made those numbers; flat/generic mail performs poorly.

The 48-hour surround, run on every completed design/build job:

  1. Yard sign, small and understated — placed at contract signing (with permission secured in the contract, alongside photo rights), stays 2–4 weeks post-completion. Company name, named crew lead's first name, one number. No wrap-truck energy — affluent buyers read over-advertising as a red flag.
  2. The "#12 piece" — a personalized one-pager to the 10–20 nearest/comparable homes, delivered within 48 hrs of completion while the crew and the fresh result are still visible:
    • Front: photo of the just-finished neighbor project (with client permission) — "We just finished at [#12 / the corner of X]."
    • Back: an AI render of the recipient's own yard — "here's what we did at #12; here's what's possible at yours." One idea, one image, tasteful.
    • CTA: named person, direct line, same-day-response promise, complimentary walkthrough.
    • This is the Scaped.ai mechanic upgraded with real adjacent social proof — the two highest-converting ingredients in the corpus (proximity proof + own-house personalization) in one piece.
  3. The crew-lead walk — final day, the crew lead personally knocks the 3–5 immediately adjacent doors: "Just finished your neighbor's yard — here's my card if anything's ever on your mind." The finished lawn "is literally your billboard at that point."
  4. Nextdoor posture:
    • Ask the happy client (at the same walkthrough moment as the review/referral ask) to post the before/after on Nextdoor and tag Creekside — a resident post outperforms any business post.
    • Claim/maintain the business page; be the first response when anyone in Dunwoody/Sandy Springs asks for a landscaper rec — Nextdoor is the #1 ask-for-recommendation channel and first-response wins. Standing daily check, or alerting, owned by a named person.
    • Post render→real pairs: the delivered AI render next to the finished install. Directly answers the documented "I'm so tired of seeing renders as the end result" skepticism, and nobody else in the market is doing it.

Measurement plan

Attribution discipline (the corpus is blunt: most landscapers don't track CAC and dump money into "what feels productive"):

  • Every lead gets a source tag at first contact — non-negotiable CRM field: past-client-referral / dormant-A / dormant-B / dormant-C / partner-<name> / surround-sign / surround-hanger / nextdoor / other.
  • Partner leads self-attribute via the co-branded page (each page is partner-tagged). Surround pieces carry the named person + a distinct "mention the [street name] project" hook. Everything else is asked and logged: "how did you hear about us?" — every time.
  • Weekly one-page rollup: leads, consults, closes, revenue, and program cost by source → cost per closed job by channel. Double down quarterly on what the sheet says, not what feels productive.

SLA metrics (leading indicators — these are controllable):

  • % of referral/partner/dormant responses contacted same day by the named person — target ≥95%.
  • % of completed jobs receiving the full 48-hr surround (sign + one-pager + crew walk) — target 100% of design/build completions.
  • % of completed jobs where Ask #1 (review + referral) was made — target 100%.
  • Render turnaround: walkthrough → proposal page ≤48 hrs, every time.

90-day "what good looks like" (all figures are estimates, for calibration not judgment):

  • Dormant campaign: full A-segment mailed; 10–20% response rate across A/B/C (est. — win-back norms vary widely); 3–8 walkthroughs booked; 1–3 closed jobs (est.). At a $25K+ average ticket, one closed job funds the entire program for the year.
  • Partner web: NAPS agreement in place + 3–5 total active partners across tiers; first co-branded proposal pages delivered; ≥1 partner-sourced closed job (est.).
  • Past-customer loop: ask made on 100% of completions; 2–5 tracked referral leads (est.) — this channel compounds and will underwhelm in 90 days; judge it at 12 months.
  • Surround: run on 100% of completions; 1–3% response on #12 pieces (est., vs <1% cold-flyer baseline; Scaped's 4.5% is the personalized-mail ceiling).
  • Baseline to beat: 80% of a healthy small shop's work comes from referrals + Google. The 90-day question is not "did referrals explode" but "is every mechanic firing on every job, and is attribution clean."

Open decisions for the owners

  1. Crew continuity — what can we honestly claim? Did Scott's crews substantially carry over? This gates the "same crews you'd recognize" line in every dormant-base message. If continuity is partial, decide the honest formulation now ("many of the same crews," "same field leadership," or drop it) — the reactivation drafts change accordingly.
  2. Partner compensation form for Tier 1. Percentage (5% of closed contract, the trade norm's floor) vs. flat per-closed-job vs. pure reciprocity + co-branding. Test the question directly in the first NAPS conversation — some operators find fee paperwork grimy and prefer the render capability + reciprocal flow; others expect the 5–10% norm on a $40K referral. Whatever is agreed must be explicit up front (the undisclosed-fee trust-breaker cuts both ways).
  3. Who is the named person? The corpus rewards specific humans: is the referral face the owner (owner-led, scales poorly, strongest trust), the crew leads (scales, matches the "Ignacio and his crew" pattern, needs buy-in + spiff structure), or a designer hire if/when one exists? This decision sets whose name goes on every card, sign, page, and SLA — and it should be one consistent answer per client relationship, not a rotating cast.
