Reading category (f) against Dima's pricing

U.S. Lawns' and Weed Man's royalties (4-6%, effectively lower at scale) buy a franchisee only a brand license and system playbook — zero selling effort, zero marketing execution, zero lead origination from the franchisor. That's the real floor for "a percentage that reflects doing nothing but owning the relationship." Dima's role — originating the targeting data, producing individualized creative, running the outreach, and building the attribution proof — is categorically more active than a franchise royalty. If his effective % lands near 4-6%, he is pricing himself at "logo license" rates for work that includes lead generation, creative production, and closing support. This is a useful ceiling-flip: category (f) should push the negotiating floor UP, not down.

The 5% / 10% claim — verdict

Client's claim: "5% of revenue is standard for a full-cycle salesperson in landscaping, 10% only with project management."

Verdict: Partially supported, with the wrong comparison class.

What's supported:

  • The direction is well evidenced: taking on project-management/delivery responsibility does correlate with higher commission across every trade analog found — roofing (7% → 10-12%), general construction/landscape-design commentary (10% → 15-20%). Higher responsibility = higher %.
  • A concrete 5%/10% split does exist in real landscape-industry data: Weed Man Virginia pays inside sales 5% and outside field sales 10%. This is the single closest real-world precedent to the client's numbers.

What's not supported, or contradicted:

  • In every source found, the "PM" version of the commission is presented as the low end of the higher band, not a ceiling: roofing's PM/materials rate tops out at 10-12% (not just "10%"); the landscape-design commentary puts PM-inclusive commission at 15-20%, well above 10%. If anything, "10% only with PM" undersells what PM-inclusive comp typically reaches elsewhere in trades.
  • The one direct 5%/10% precedent found (Weed Man VA) splits on inside vs. outside sales difficulty, not on full-cycle vs. project-management — a different axis than the client's framing. Applying it to defend "10% only with PM" is not quite what that source says.
  • The most-repeated landscape-specific figure — "~8% of what they sell" as total blended comp (salary + commission combined) — sits almost exactly between the client's 5% and 10%, which is a reasonable sanity check but also shows 5% alone (with no base salary implied) is on the low side of what full-cycle landscape sales performers typically clear once salary is counted.
  • Real operator variance is wide: Planted Earth Landscaping pays 1-2.5% margin-tiered commission on total sale value — far below either 5% or 10%, undercutting "5% is standard" as a single fixed number.

The more important point — category mismatch: All of category (b) benchmarks an internal, W-2 salesperson — someone on the company's payroll, with a base salary, benefits, a vehicle, and no origination responsibility (leads/marketing are supplied to them). That is not Dima's role. He carries no base salary, no benefits cost to Arjun, no employment relationship — and unlike an internal salesperson, he also builds the targeting system, produces the creative, runs outreach, and provides attribution proof. That combination puts his economics closer to categories (c) trades referral fees (5-10%, up to 15% for specialty trades), (d) performance/rev-share marketing deals (10-30% of attributed revenue), and the ceiling-flip read of (f) franchise royalties (4-6% buys zero selling/marketing effort) — not category (b) internal sales comp.

Net: the client's 5%/10% framing is a legitimate opening reference and worth acknowledging as directionally real (the PM-raises-commission logic holds up), but it imports the wrong comparison class. The existing recommendation — pilot at $1,500/mo + 3-4%, converting to a $4-6K/mo floor credited against 10% on evidence (from 2026-07-21_Fee-Structure-Research-and-Pressure-Test.md) — already sits at or below the low end of what referral-fee and rev-share norms would support (c/d), while still landing inside the client's own comfort band if they insist on framing this as (b)-style sales comp. No change to the existing recommendation is warranted by this research; it strengthens the case for holding 10% rather than conceding to a flat 5%.

Gaps

  • No landscaping-specific rev-share/performance-marketing deal was found in the wild (category d figures are generic digital-marketing content, not trades-specific) — if Dima can find even one actual performance-marketing landscaping case study, it would meaningfully upgrade this category's confidence.
